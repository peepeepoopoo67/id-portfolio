Process

Three passes, every project

Every lesson below went through the same sequence, not as a checklist, but because skipping a pass is exactly where courses go generic.

  1. 01

    Diagnose

    Find the actual gap: not "learners don't know X," but the specific moment they misapply what they already know. That moment becomes the lesson's hook.

  2. 02

    Draft

    Structure before polish. Every block earns its place: does it build understanding, or just restate it? Cut anything that only decorates.

  3. 03

    Pressure-test

    Run it against a scenario a real learner would face, not a generic knowledge check. If the assessment doesn't require the judgment call, rewrite the assessment.

Case Study 01

AI in Education: A Double-Edged Sword

From AI for Equity: Addressing Bias and Accessibility in AI-Driven Learning (Module 1: Introduction to AI, Equity, and Accessibility)

Audience Educators, curriculum designers, tech coordinators, ed leaders
Format Framing lesson: paired benefit/risk model
Sits between Module 1 Objectives → Equity vs. Equality (Language)

The problem

Most educators walk in already on a side: sold on AI's efficiency, or already braced for what it'll break. Neither position is any good for evaluating an actual tool. This lesson is the first one in the course, so its real job isn't "AI has pros and cons"; it's installing the idea that a capability and its risk are the same mechanism, not two separate lists. That's the lens every later lesson in the module (bias, accessibility, ethics) depends on.

The approach

Skip the balanced listicle: it reads as neutral and teaches nothing. Instead, pair each benefit directly with the risk it creates by the same mechanism: personalization is also sorting, automation is also opacity. Nothing gets resolved here; that's deliberate. This lesson hands the learner the paired frame, then the rest of the module spends its budget investigating one pair at a time.

Structure, block by block

  1. HookTwo real short cases side by side: an AI tool that caught a struggling reader early, and one that flagged a student unfairly. No framework yet, just stakes.
  2. ConceptPaired benefit/risk model: same capability, two outcomes depending on who built it and how.
  3. ReflectShort prompt: name one AI tool already in your building and identify its own double edge.
  4. BridgeExplicit hand-off to what's coming (language, bias, accessibility) framed as this pair examined one at a time.

Paired benefit/risk model: the comparison as built in Rise

The upside
Personalized pacing catches gaps early
Automated grading returns feedback in seconds
Adaptive content meets each learner where they are
The catch
Same system can quietly track students onto fixed paths
Same speed can be opaque and hard to appeal
Same adaptivity inherits whatever bias trained it

note: each row in "the catch" is caused by the exact mechanism in the matching row of "the upside", not a separate downside. That 1:1 pairing is the whole teaching point.

Case Study 02

Raw Data Interpretation Practice

From Special Education Advocacy: Professional Practice, Evidence, and Facilitation (Module 3: Evidence Analysis and the Blueprint)

Audience Non-attorney special ed advocates
Format Applied worksheet, deliverable as assessment
Sits between Progress-monitoring data → Three-List Synthesis

The problem

This module treats the IEP as a functional blueprint: services and goals have to be traceable back to data, not intuition. Most advocates entering the field come from lived experience, not formal data training, so the risk isn't that they ignore the data; it's that they either over-read it into a diagnosis that isn't theirs to make, or under-use it and fall back on impression. Both produce a recommendation nobody can defend in a meeting.

The approach

This is a skills-practice lesson, not a knowledge lesson, so it isn't built around a quiz. The learner is handed three months of raw progress-monitoring data and walked, prompt by prompt, from pattern to a recommendation they'd actually be willing to say out loud in an IEP meeting. The worksheet itself is the assessment.

Structure, block by block

  1. StakesShort framing block: what a weak interpretation costs the family in the room.
  2. DataThree-month progress-monitoring set, presented as the advocate would actually receive it.
  3. GuidedStructured worksheet prompts: pattern → gap → recommendation, one step at a time.
  4. CompareExemplar vs. weak recommendation, side by side, for self-assessment.
  5. No quizThe completed recommendation is the evidence of learning, matching how the syllabus itself scores this lesson.

Sample progress-monitoring data, as presented in the worksheet

Month Goal Area Target Observed Trend
1Reading fluency65 wcpm52 wcpmflat
2Reading fluency68 wcpm54 wcpmflat
3Reading fluency71 wcpm55 wcpmflat

Flat trend across three consecutive points: the pattern the worksheet trains learners to name before recommending a service change.

note: the exemplar/weak comparison is the real teaching moment: it shows the difference between "reading isn't improving" and a recommendation specific enough to act on.
